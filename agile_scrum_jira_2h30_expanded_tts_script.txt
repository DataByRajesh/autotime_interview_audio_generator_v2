Section 1. Agile.

Terminology.

Agile means flexible and adaptive. In software delivery, Agile means delivering work in small useful pieces, getting feedback, and improving continuously.

Agile is not one meeting. Agile is not one tool. Agile is not only Jira. Agile is not only speed. Agile is a delivery mindset.

Concept.

Agile is a way of building and delivering software step by step. The team does not try to build everything at once. The team breaks work into smaller useful parts. The team builds one part, tests it, shows it to users, gets feedback, improves it, then moves to the next part.

The Agile cycle is:

Build small.
Test early.
Get feedback.
Improve.
Repeat.

Repeat that.

Build small.
Test early.
Get feedback.
Improve.
Repeat.

This is the heart of Agile. Agile is a way to reduce delivery risk. It reduces the risk of building the wrong thing.

In old-style delivery, sometimes called waterfall, the team may spend months gathering requirements. Then they write a large requirement document. Then developers build the full system. Then testers test near the end. Then business users see the system very late.

The risk is that after many months, business users may say: This is not what we wanted. This report has the wrong fields. This workflow is too slow. This dashboard does not show the exception we need. The system is technically complete, but not useful.

Agile tries to avoid this. Agile says: do not wait until the end to learn whether the solution is useful. Show smaller parts earlier. Get feedback earlier. Improve earlier. Deliver business value earlier.

Example.

Imagine a company has a payment system. Finance operations cannot easily identify successful payments that have no settlement record. They also cannot easily see payment and settlement amount mismatches. They cannot easily identify duplicate payment references. They rely on manual spreadsheet checks.

The business problem is: Finance operations need a better way to identify payment reconciliation exceptions.

If the team tries to build everything at once, the dashboard may become too large. It may include missing settlements, amount mismatches, duplicate payments, refunds, chargebacks, filters, export, audit logs, user permissions, management summary and daily alerts. That is too much for one delivery.

Agile breaks it down.

Sprint one: show successful payments without settlement records.
Sprint two: show payment and settlement amount mismatches.
Sprint three: add date, currency and payment method filters.
Sprint four: add export.
Sprint five: add audit log.
Sprint six: add management summary.

This is Agile. Small useful delivery. Early feedback. Continuous improvement.

Repeat the concept.

Agile means small delivery, early feedback and continuous improvement. Agile does not mean chaos. Agile does not mean no planning. Agile does not mean no documentation. Agile means flexible structure.

Memory line.

Agile is flexible structure.

Interview answer.

Agile is an iterative way of delivering software in smaller increments. Instead of waiting until the end of a large project, Agile teams deliver useful parts regularly, get feedback and adapt. From a Business Analyst perspective, Agile helps turn business needs into user stories, acceptance criteria and testable requirements throughout delivery.

Memory lock.

Agile reduces risk by creating early feedback.

Section 2. Business Analyst in Agile.

Terminology.

A Business Analyst, or BA, is the person who helps understand the business problem and convert it into clear requirements for the delivery team. In Agile, the BA is a clarity creator.

Concept.

The BA connects business and technology. The BA helps business users explain the real problem. The BA helps developers understand what to build. The BA helps testers understand what to test. The BA helps the Product Owner prepare clear backlog items.

The BA creates user stories, acceptance criteria, process flows, UAT scenarios and defect descriptions.

Example.

Business says: We need better reconciliation.

This is vague. The BA should not directly create a Jira ticket called “Improve reconciliation”.

The BA asks: What reconciliation issue are you facing? Are successful payments missing settlement records? Are settlement amounts different from payment amounts? Are there duplicate payments? Which team is affected? What fields should the report show? What filters are needed? What is the business impact? What does success look like?

Repeat the concept.

The BA does not just write notes. The BA does not just attend meetings. The BA does not just take minutes. The BA creates clarity. The BA turns vague business language into clear, testable delivery work.

Payment example.

Business problem: Successful payments are missing settlement records.

The BA asks: What status means successful? Is it SUCCESS, COMPLETED or CAPTURED? Where is settlement data stored? Is settlement immediate or batch-based? How long should we wait before calling settlement missing? Which users need access? Should the report show total affected amount? Should export be allowed? Should export be audited?

Now the BA has moved from vague requirement to clear delivery logic.

Interview answer.

As a Business Analyst in Agile, I help translate business problems into clear user stories, acceptance criteria, process flows and testable requirements. I work with the Product Owner, developers, testers and stakeholders to clarify scope, business rules, edge cases and expected outcomes.

Memory lock.

The BA turns vague problems into clear, testable work.

Section 3. Scrum.

Terminology.

Scrum is an Agile framework. A framework means a structured way of working. Scrum gives the team roles, ceremonies and a delivery rhythm.

Concept.

Scrum organises work into short delivery cycles called sprints. The team prepares work, plans the sprint, builds, tests, reviews, improves, then repeats. Scrum helps Agile become practical.

Agile is the idea. Scrum is the routine. Agile is the mindset. Scrum is the framework.

Example.

The team wants to build a missing settlement report. In Scrum, this work is written as a user story. The story is refined. The story is planned into a sprint. Developers build it. Testers test it. The BA clarifies questions. The team reviews it at the end of the sprint. Stakeholders give feedback. The next sprint improves the product.

Repeat the concept.

Scrum gives Agile structure. Scrum uses sprints. Scrum uses roles. Scrum uses ceremonies. Scrum helps the team deliver work regularly.

Interview answer.

Scrum is an Agile framework that organises work into sprints. It includes roles such as Product Owner, Scrum Master and Development Team, and ceremonies such as sprint planning, daily stand-up, backlog refinement, sprint review and retrospective. As a BA, I support Scrum by clarifying requirements, preparing stories, defining acceptance criteria and supporting delivery and UAT.

Memory lock.

Scrum gives Agile a working rhythm.

Section 4. Sprint.

Terminology.

A sprint is a fixed delivery period. It is a short time window where the team works on selected backlog items. A sprint is usually one to four weeks. Two weeks is common.

Concept.

During a sprint, the team should build, test and complete agreed work. The sprint should contain clear and ready work. If work is unclear, developers and testers may get blocked. That is why backlog refinement is important before sprint planning.

Example.

Sprint goal: Deliver missing settlement report.

Sprint work may include: create backend query, build API endpoint, create report screen, add date filter, add currency filter, prepare test data and validate with SQL.

Repeat the concept.

A sprint is a short delivery window. A sprint contains selected work. A sprint should produce tested output. A sprint should not contain unclear work.

Interview answer.

A sprint is a fixed delivery period where the team works on selected backlog items. At the end of the sprint, the aim is to deliver completed and tested work that meets the agreed acceptance criteria.

Memory lock.

A sprint is a short delivery window with agreed work.

Who? Finance operations analyst. What? View successful payments without settlement records. Why? To investigate reconciliation exceptions quickly.

Repeat the concept.

User story equals who, what and why.

A strong story says: As a finance operations analyst, I want to view successful payments without settlement records so that I can investigate reconciliation exceptions quickly.

More examples.

As a payment support analyst, I want to view failed payments with customer details and failure reason so that I can investigate customer complaints faster.

As a compliance analyst, I want to see customers with missing KYC status so that I can investigate onboarding risk.

A bug is a defect or issue in the system.

Concept.

A bug means actual behaviour does not match expected behaviour. A good bug needs evidence and business impact.

Example.

Expected result: Successful payment should create settlement record.

Actual result: Payment is SUCCESS, but settlement record is missing.

Evidence: Payment ID 105 has no settlement record.

Business impact: Finance operations cannot reconcile the payment.

Repeat the concept.

Bug means something is wrong. Bug means expected and actual are different. Bug needs evidence. Bug needs impact.

Weak bug: Payment not working.

Strong bug: Payment ID 105 has status SUCCESS, but no matching settlement record exists. Expected result is settlement creation. Actual result is missing settlement. Business impact is reconciliation failure.

Interview answer.

A good bug report should include expected result, actual result, steps to reproduce, evidence and business impact. This helps developers and testers investigate quickly.

Memory lock.

Good bug equals expected, actual, evidence and impact.

Section 23. Acceptance Criteria.

Terminology.

Acceptance criteria are the conditions that must be met for a user story to be accepted.

Concept.

Acceptance criteria make a story testable. They explain expected behaviour. They reduce misunderstanding between business, developers and testers.

Example.

User story: As a finance operations analyst, I want to view successful payments without settlement records so that I can investigate reconciliation exceptions quickly.

Acceptance criteria: Only SUCCESS payments should appear. Only payments with no matching settlement should appear. The report should show payment ID, reference, customer ID, amount, currency, date and payment method. The user should filter by date, currency and payment method. If no records exist, show “No missing settlements found”. The count should match SQL validation.

Now understand why each rule matters.

Only SUCCESS payments should appear. This avoids showing failed payments as settlement exceptions.

Only payments with no matching settlement should appear. This is the core business rule.

The report should show payment ID and reference. This helps users identify the transaction.

The report should show amount and currency. This helps users understand financial impact.

The report should show date. This helps users prioritise older exceptions.

Filters help investigation. No-records message avoids confusion. SQL validation confirms data accuracy.

Repeat the concept.

Acceptance criteria make stories clear. Acceptance criteria make stories testable. Acceptance criteria reduce misunderstanding.

Bad acceptance criteria: System should work properly. Dashboard should be user-friendly. Data should be accurate.

Good acceptance criteria: Only SUCCESS payments with no settlement should appear. Report count should match SQL validation. User can filter by date range and currency. If no records exist, show “No missing settlements found”.

Interview answer.

Acceptance criteria should be clear, specific and testable. They define what must be true for the story to be accepted by the business.

Memory lock.

Story gives the goal. Acceptance criteria give the rules.

Another example: Given a payment has status FAILED, when the user opens the missing settlement report, then the payment should not appear in the report.

Another example: Given no missing settlement records exist, when the user opens the report, then the system should display “No missing settlements found”.

Repeat the concept.

Given is setup. When is action. Then is result. Given When Then makes requirements easier to test.

Interview answer.

Given When Then helps structure acceptance criteria into precondition, action and expected result. It is useful because it makes requirements easier to test.

Memory lock.

Given is setup. When is action. Then is result.

Section 27. UAT.

Terminology.

Another UAT scenario. Requirement: Missing settlement report should show successful payments with no settlement. Steps: open missing settlement report, apply date filter, search payment reference. Expected result: payment appears if it is successful and settlement is missing.

Another UAT scenario. Requirement: Successful payment with settlement should not appear. Steps: open report and search payment reference. Expected result: payment does not appear because settlement exists.

Repeat the concept.

UAT confirms business acceptance. UAT checks real user needs. UAT confirms whether the solution works for the business process.

Section 28. SQL Validation in Agile.

Terminology.

SQL validation means using SQL queries to check whether system data matches the expected business rule.

Concept.

For a Business Systems Analyst, SQL helps validate reports, UAT results and defects.

Example.

Acceptance criteria says: Only successful payments without settlements should appear.

SQL validation logic: use payments table, left join settlements table, filter payment status SUCCESS and find records where settlement ID is NULL.

In simple business language, this means compare successful payment records with settlement records and show cases where settlement is missing.

Repeat the concept.

Agile defines the need. Jira tracks the work. Acceptance criteria define expected behaviour. SQL proves the data. UAT confirms business value.

Interview answer.

SQL can support Agile delivery by validating data, checking UAT results, investigating defects and confirming whether the delivered feature matches business rules.

SQL proves the data.

The BA asks: Is it urgent? Is it in scope? Does it affect sprint work? Does it need Product Owner approval? Does it affect testing?

Repeat the concept.

Agile accepts change. Change needs impact assessment. Change needs prioritisation. Change should not silently break sprint scope.

Interview answer.

I handle change requests by clarifying the need, assessing impact and discussing priority with the Product Owner and delivery team. If it is urgent, sprint scope may be adjusted. If not, it can go into the backlog for future prioritisation.

Change is welcome, but impact must be managed.

Section 31. Good Jira Story Ticket.

Terminology.

A Jira ticket is a work item in Jira. A story ticket represents a user-focused requirement.

Concept.

A good Jira story ticket should include enough information for developers to build and testers to validate. It should include title, business context, user story, acceptance criteria, data rules, dependencies, assumptions, out of scope, testing notes, priority and linked epic.

Example.

Ticket type: Story.
Title: Show successful payments missing settlement records.
Epic: Payment reconciliation dashboard.

Business context: Finance operations currently perform manual checks to identify successful payments that do not have matching settlement records. This delays reconciliation and increases operational risk. The team needs a report that highlights successful payments where settlement is missing.

User story: As a finance operations analyst, I want to view successful payments without settlement records so that I can investigate reconciliation exceptions quickly.

Acceptance criteria: Only payments with status SUCCESS should be included. Payments should appear only where no matching settlement record exists. The report should display payment ID, payment reference, customer ID, amount, currency, payment date and payment method. The user should be able to filter by date range, currency and payment method. The report should display total record count and total affected amount. If no records exist, show “No missing settlements found”. The result count should match SQL validation.

Data rules: Payment status must be read from the payments table. Settlement match should be based on payment ID or payment reference, depending on confirmed system design. Pending settlements within the expected processing window should be excluded if the business confirms a delay rule.

Dependencies: Settlement data source must be available. Payment-to-settlement matching rule must be confirmed. Test data must include successful payments with and without settlements.

Assumptions: Finance operations users have permission to view payment exception records. The first version does not include refunds or chargebacks.

Out of scope: Refund exceptions. Chargeback exceptions. Automated alert emails. Audit log for export.

Testing notes: QA should validate records against SQL query. UAT should be completed by finance operations user.

Priority: High, because reconciliation delay affects finance operations.

Repeat the concept.

A good Jira ticket does not only say what to build. It explains why it matters. It gives business context. It gives testable rules. It highlights dependencies. It reduces assumptions. It helps developers and testers.

Interview answer.

A good Jira ticket explains what is needed, why it matters and how it will be accepted. I include business context, user story, acceptance criteria, data rules, dependencies and testing notes so developers and testers do not need to guess.

Memory lock.

Good Jira ticket equals context, story, rules and tests.

Section 32. Jira Bug Ticket.

Terminology.

A bug ticket documents a defect. A defect means actual behaviour does not match expected behaviour.

Concept.

A good bug ticket should include title, environment, steps to reproduce, expected result, actual result, evidence, affected records, severity, priority, business impact and possible root cause if known.

Example.

Ticket type: Bug.
Title: Successful payment PAY1005 missing settlement record.
Environment: UAT environment.
Module: Payment reconciliation.

Steps to reproduce: open the payments table or payment screen. Search for payment reference PAY1005. Confirm payment status is SUCCESS. Search settlement records using payment ID or payment reference. No settlement record is found.

Expected result: A payment with status SUCCESS should have a matching settlement record after the expected processing window.

Actual result: Payment PAY1005 has status SUCCESS, but no matching settlement record exists.

Evidence: SQL validation shows payment exists in payments table and no matching settlement exists in settlements table.

Affected record: PAY1005.

Business impact: Finance operations cannot reconcile this payment. If the issue affects multiple payments, daily reconciliation and reporting may be delayed.

Severity: High, if multiple successful payments are missing settlements.

Priority: High, if this blocks UAT sign-off or month-end reconciliation.

Notes: Need to confirm whether settlement should be immediate or batch-based.

Repeat the concept.

A weak bug says payment not working. A strong bug says what was expected, what actually happened, how to reproduce it, which record is affected, what evidence exists and what business impact it creates.

Interview answer.

For bug tickets, I provide expected result, actual result, steps to reproduce, evidence, affected records and business impact. This helps the team triage and fix issues faster.

Memory lock.

Bug ticket needs expected, actual, evidence and impact.

Section 33. Defect Triage.

Terminology.

Defect triage is the process of reviewing, prioritising and assigning defects. Triage means deciding how serious the issue is, how urgent it is and what should happen next.

Concept.

In defect triage, the team may ask: Is this a real defect? Is it expected behaviour? What is the severity? What is the priority? Who should investigate it? Does it block release? Does it block UAT? Does it affect customers? Does it affect finance, compliance or reporting?

Example.

Bug: Successful payment missing settlement.

In triage, the team asks: Is settlement expected immediately? Is there a batch delay? Is the payment actually successful? Is the settlement created in a different system? Is this one record or many records? Is the amount high? Does it affect reporting? Does it block UAT?

Repeat the concept.

A BA helps triage by clarifying business impact. The BA does not always fix the bug. The BA helps explain why the bug matters. The BA confirms expected behaviour. The BA helps decide whether it is truly a defect or expected system design.

Scenario.

Developer says: The missing settlement is expected because settlement is batch-based.

BA response: The BA should clarify the business rule. If settlement is expected after batch processing, the acceptance criteria should include the processing window. The report should show only records missing settlement after the allowed settlement delay.

Interview answer.

In defect triage, I help clarify expected behaviour, business impact and acceptance criteria. I also help determine whether the issue is a true defect, a data issue, a requirement gap or expected behaviour.

Memory lock.

Triage decides seriousness, urgency and next action.

Section 42. Complete Agile BA Flow.

Terminology.

Agile BA flow means the full journey from business problem to accepted solution.

Concept.

A vague business issue becomes a clear user story. The user story becomes a Jira ticket. The Jira ticket becomes sprint work. Sprint work becomes tested feature. Tested feature becomes UAT. UAT becomes business acceptance.

Example.

Step one: clarify the problem. Who is affected? Finance operations. What is the issue? Successful payments may not have settlement records. Why does it matter? Finance cannot reconcile the payments. What happens today? Manual spreadsheet checking.

Step two: define the business rule. If payment status is SUCCESS, then settlement should exist after the expected processing window.

Step three: write user story. As a finance operations analyst, I want to view successful payments without settlement records so that I can investigate reconciliation exceptions quickly.

Step four: write acceptance criteria. Only SUCCESS payments should be considered. Only records with no settlement should appear. Fields should include payment ID, reference, customer ID, amount, currency, date and payment method. Filters should include date range, currency and payment method. If no records exist, show a no-records message. Count should match SQL validation.

Step five: create Jira ticket. Include title, context, user story, acceptance criteria, data rules, dependencies, priority and testing notes.

Step six: refine the backlog item. Discuss data source, settlement timing, filters, permissions, edge cases and test data.

Step seven: plan into sprint. Team confirms the story is ready and estimates effort.

Step eight: develop. Developers build. BA clarifies questions.

Step nine: test. Testers validate against acceptance criteria.

Step ten: validate with SQL. SQL confirms report data matches backend rule.

Step eleven: complete UAT. Finance operations confirms the report helps investigation.

Step twelve: close as done. Product Owner accepts. Story is closed.

Repeat the concept.

Problem becomes story. Story becomes ticket. Ticket becomes delivery. Delivery becomes test. Test becomes business acceptance.

Interview answer.

I would manage a payment reconciliation feature by clarifying the business problem, breaking it into smaller stories, writing acceptance criteria, creating Jira tickets, supporting refinement and sprint delivery, helping testers, validating data using SQL and supporting UAT with finance users.

Section 47. Waterfall versus Agile.

Terminology.

Waterfall is a project delivery approach where work usually moves in a sequence.

Requirements first.

Design next.

Development next.

Testing next.

Deployment next.

The word waterfall gives the idea of water flowing down step by step.

Once one stage is complete, the next stage starts.

Agile is different.

Agile delivers in smaller increments.

Agile gets feedback earlier.

Concept.

Waterfall can work when requirements are stable, clear and unlikely to change.

For example, if a company is doing a fixed compliance change with clearly defined rules and fixed deadline, a more structured waterfall-style approach may work.

But many software products are uncertain.

Users may not know exactly what they need until they see something.

Business priorities may change.

Data rules may become clearer during analysis.

Technology constraints may appear during development.

That is where Agile helps.

Agile allows learning during delivery.

Example.

Finance operations says:

We need a reconciliation dashboard.

In a waterfall approach, the team may collect all dashboard requirements first, build everything, test everything and show users near the end.

In Agile, the team can deliver the missing settlement report first.

Finance users test it.

They give feedback.

Then the team adds amount mismatches.

Then filters.

Then export.

Then audit log.

Repeat the concept.

Waterfall is sequential.

Agile is iterative.

Waterfall tries to define more upfront.

Agile learns through small delivery and feedback.

Interview answer.

Waterfall is a sequential approach where requirements, design, development and testing happen in larger stages. Agile is iterative, where the team delivers smaller increments, gets feedback and adapts. As a BA, I can work with structured documentation where needed, but Agile helps me validate requirements earlier with business users.

Memory line.

Waterfall is step-by-step phases. Agile is step-by-step learning.

Section 48. Kanban.

Terminology.

Kanban is an Agile method focused on visualising work and managing flow.

Kanban often uses a board with columns like:

To Do.

In Progress.

In Review.

Testing.

Done.

Concept.

Kanban is useful when work arrives continuously.

For example, production support issues, application support tasks, small enhancements or operational requests.

Scrum uses fixed sprints.

Kanban focuses more on continuous flow.

Kanban may limit how much work can be in progress at one time.

This is called WIP limit.

WIP means work in progress.

A WIP limit prevents the team from starting too many things and finishing too few.

Example.

An Application Analyst team receives ongoing tickets:

Payment failure investigation.

Settlement mismatch issue.

User access issue.

Report data issue.

Small dashboard enhancement.

Instead of planning everything into two-week sprints, the team may use Kanban to move tickets from To Do to In Progress to Testing to Done.

Velocity shows delivery trend.

Velocity helps planning.

Velocity is not a guarantee.

Interview answer.

Velocity is the amount of work a team completes in a sprint, often measured in story points. It helps with planning and forecasting, but it should be used carefully because it is a team learning metric, not a pressure tool.

Velocity helps forecast, not force.

Dependency is needed from others.
