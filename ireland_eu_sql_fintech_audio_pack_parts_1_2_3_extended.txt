1. Database tables, rows, and columns

A database stores structured data.
The data is stored in tables.
A table has rows and columns.
A row is one record.
A column is one field.

In a payment system, a customers table may store customer ID, name, email, country, KYC status, and created date.
A payments table may store payment ID, customer ID, payment reference, amount, currency, payment method, status, and payment date.
A settlements table may store settlement ID, payment ID, settlement amount, settlement status, and settlement date.
A refunds table may store refund ID, payment ID, refund amount, refund status, and refund date.
A chargebacks table may store chargeback ID, payment ID, dispute reason, chargeback amount, and case status.
A ledger table may store accounting entries.
An audit log may store who changed what and when.
A status history table may store the movement from initiated, authorised, captured, successful, failed, settled, refunded, or disputed.

The important interview point is that one business event normally creates data across multiple tables.
When a customer makes a payment, the payment table may store the initial transaction, the status history may store status changes, the settlement table may store settlement, the ledger may store financial postings, and the audit log may store system actions.

So I should not think only table by table.
I should think process by process.
The business process creates data.
SQL helps me read that process from the database.

In interview language, I can say:

I use SQL to understand the data trail behind a business process. For example, a payment may create records in payment, settlement, ledger, status history, and audit tables. By checking those related tables, I can understand whether the process completed correctly or where it failed.

2. Primary keys and foreign keys

A primary key uniquely identifies a record.
For example, customer_id uniquely identifies one customer.
payment_id uniquely identifies one payment.
settlement_id uniquely identifies one settlement.

A foreign key links one table to another.
For example, customer_id in the payments table links a payment to a customer.
payment_id in the settlements table links a settlement to a payment.

This matters because joins depend on correct keys.
If I join using the wrong key, I may create wrong results and wrong conclusions.

Business example:

If a payment has customer_id 10, then customer_id 10 should exist in the customers table.
If not, the payment is an orphan record.
That can cause reporting, support, compliance, and customer ownership problems.

Interview answer:

A primary key uniquely identifies a record. A foreign key connects records across tables. In analyst work, understanding keys is important because SQL joins must reflect the real system relationship. Wrong joins can lead to wrong business conclusions.

3. SELECT — choosing useful data

SELECT chooses which columns I want to see.
A beginner may write:

SELECT * FROM payments;

This shows every column.
But in professional analysis, I should usually select only the fields needed for the business question.

Example:

SELECT payment_id, payment_reference, customer_id, amount, currency, status, payment_date
FROM payments;

The technical purpose is to retrieve specific columns.
The business purpose is to see the core facts of the transaction.
The system purpose is to identify the payment record that represents the event.

In an interview, I should say:

I avoid selecting unnecessary columns where possible. I select the fields needed for the investigation, such as payment ID, reference, customer ID, amount, currency, status, and date. This keeps the query focused and easier to explain.

4. WHERE — filtering the business question

WHERE filters records.
In real systems, tables may contain millions of rows.
I rarely want everything.
I want records that match the business question.

Example:

SELECT payment_id, payment_reference, amount, status
FROM payments
WHERE status = 'FAILED';

Technical explanation:
This returns only failed payment rows.

Business explanation:
This helps identify transactions that did not complete.

System explanation:
This shows where the payment process stopped or failed.

Interview explanation:

WHERE is useful when I need to investigate a specific condition, such as failed payments, pending payments, high-value payments, a specific customer, or a specific incident window.

5. AND, OR, and IN

AND means all conditions must be true.

Example:

SELECT payment_id, amount, status
FROM payments
WHERE status = 'SUCCESS'
AND amount > 1000;

This shows successful payments above 1000.

OR means one of the conditions can be true.

Example:

SELECT payment_id, status
FROM payments
WHERE status = 'FAILED'
OR status = 'PENDING';

A cleaner version is IN:

SELECT payment_id, status
FROM payments
WHERE status IN ('FAILED', 'PENDING');

Interview explanation:

I use AND when all business conditions must match. I use OR or IN when the business question includes multiple categories, such as failed or pending payments.

6. SQL clause order

The common SQL order is:

SELECT.
FROM.
JOIN.
WHERE.
GROUP BY.
HAVING.
ORDER BY.

Example:

SELECT status, COUNT(*) AS payment_count
FROM payments
WHERE payment_date >= '2026-06-01'
GROUP BY status
HAVING COUNT(*) > 10
ORDER BY payment_count DESC;

In plain language:

Choose columns.
Choose table.
Join related tables if needed.
Filter rows.
Group records.
Filter groups.
Sort results.

This order is important in interviews because it shows I understand query structure rather than just memorising fragments.

7. Aliases

Aliases make SQL cleaner.

FROM customers c means customers is called c.
FROM payments p means payments is called p.
FROM settlements s means settlements is called s.

Then I can write c.customer_name, p.amount, and s.settlement_amount.

Correct format:
p.customer_id.

Wrong format:
customer_id.p.

The table alias comes first.
The column name comes second.

Interview explanation:

I use aliases to keep queries readable, especially when joining several tables. It also makes it clear which table each column comes from.

8. INNER JOIN

INNER JOIN returns matching records from both tables.

Business question:
Show customer name, country, payment reference, amount, and payment status.

Customer name and country are in customers.
Payment reference and amount are in payments.
So I need to join the tables.

Query:

SELECT c.customer_name, c.country, p.payment_reference, p.amount, p.status
FROM customers c
JOIN payments p
ON c.customer_id = p.customer_id;

Technical explanation:
The join connects customers and payments using customer_id.

Business explanation:
This shows which customer made which payment.

System explanation:
This connects customer master data with transaction data.

Interview answer:

A join is used when the business question requires data from more than one table. For example, to investigate failed payments by customer or country, I join customer data with payment data using customer_id.

9. LEFT JOIN

LEFT JOIN returns all records from the left table and matching records from the right table.
If there is no match, the right table values return NULL.

This is one of the most important analyst concepts.
It helps find missing related records.

Business rule:
Every successful payment should have a settlement record.

Query:

SELECT p.payment_id, p.payment_reference, p.amount, p.status, s.settlement_id
FROM payments p
LEFT JOIN settlements s
ON p.payment_id = s.payment_id
WHERE p.status = 'SUCCESS'
AND s.settlement_id IS NULL;

Technical explanation:
Start with payments.
Left join settlements.
Keep successful payments.
Find records where settlement is missing.

Business explanation:
This identifies successful payments not settled.

System explanation:
The payment completed, but the downstream settlement record was not created.

UAT explanation:
If the requirement says successful payments must settle, this query validates that rule.

Interview answer:

I use LEFT JOIN when I need to find missing related records. In payments, it helps identify successful payments without settlements, which is important for reconciliation, UAT, and defect investigation.

10. RIGHT JOIN and FULL OUTER JOIN

RIGHT JOIN returns all records from the right table and matching records from the left table.
In practice, I can often rewrite it as a LEFT JOIN by changing table order.

FULL OUTER JOIN returns all records from both tables.
It shows matches where possible, and NULL where no match exists.

A FULL OUTER JOIN can be useful when comparing two datasets.
For example, it can show:

Payments with settlements.
Payments without settlements.
Settlements without payments.

But in most Business Analyst scenarios, INNER JOIN and LEFT JOIN cover most needs.

Interview answer:

I mainly use INNER JOIN for matching records and LEFT JOIN for missing records. I understand RIGHT JOIN and FULL OUTER JOIN, but for practical analysis, LEFT JOIN is usually enough to identify missing child or parent records.

11. NULL handling

NULL means missing, unknown, or not available.
NULL is not zero.
NULL is not blank text.
NULL means there is no value.

Correct:

WHERE status IS NULL.

Incorrect:

WHERE status = NULL.

To check available values:

WHERE status IS NOT NULL.

Business example:

SELECT payment_id, status
FROM payments
WHERE status IS NULL;

This finds payments with missing status.

System impact:
Missing status can break reporting, workflows, customer support, reconciliation, and UAT.

Interview answer:

NULL handling is important because missing values can indicate incomplete processing, failed data migration, API mapping issues, or workflow defects.

12. Mismatch checks

SQL can compare two columns.

Business rule:
Payment amount should match settlement amount unless there is a valid business reason such as fees, FX conversion, partial settlement, refund, or chargeback.

Query:

SELECT p.payment_id, p.payment_reference, p.amount AS payment_amount, s.settlement_amount, p.currency, p.status, s.settlement_status
FROM payments p
JOIN settlements s
ON p.payment_id = s.payment_id
WHERE p.amount <> s.settlement_amount;

Technical explanation:
Join payment and settlement records.
Compare amount fields.
Return mismatches.

Business explanation:
This identifies reconciliation issues.

System explanation:
The issue could come from calculation logic, data mapping, currency handling, partial settlement, or downstream integration.

Interview answer:

I would not immediately call every mismatch a defect. I would first check whether business rules explain the difference, then raise a defect if the mismatch is unexpected.

13. COUNT, SUM, AVG, MIN, and MAX

COUNT counts records.
SUM adds values.
AVG calculates average.
MIN finds the smallest value.
MAX finds the highest value.

Query:

SELECT status, COUNT(*) AS payment_count, SUM(amount) AS total_amount, AVG(amount) AS average_amount, MIN(amount) AS minimum_amount, MAX(amount) AS maximum_amount
FROM payments
GROUP BY status;

Business explanation:
This shows both volume and value by payment status.

Interview insight:
Count alone can mislead.
Ten failed payments worth 100 euros may be less urgent than two failed payments worth 20,000 euros.
A strong analyst checks both count and value.

14. GROUP BY

GROUP BY summarises rows into groups.

If I group by status, I get one result per status.
If I group by country, I get one result per country.
If I group by customer, I get one result per customer.

Example:

SELECT c.country, COUNT(*) AS failed_payment_count
FROM customers c
JOIN payments p
ON c.customer_id = p.customer_id
WHERE p.status = 'FAILED'
GROUP BY c.country;

Business explanation:
This shows failed payments by country.

System insight:
If failures are concentrated in one country, the root cause may involve local issuer behaviour, gateway routing, currency, payment method availability, or regulatory rules.

15. HAVING

WHERE filters rows before grouping.
HAVING filters groups after aggregation.

Example:

SELECT c.country, COUNT(*) AS failed_payment_count
FROM customers c
JOIN payments p
ON c.customer_id = p.customer_id
WHERE p.status = 'FAILED'
GROUP BY c.country
HAVING COUNT(*) > 10;

Business explanation:
This identifies countries with more than ten failed payments.

Interview answer:

WHERE is used before aggregation. HAVING is used after aggregation. I use HAVING when I need to filter summarised results, such as customers with repeated failures or countries with high failure counts.

16. CASE WHEN

CASE WHEN creates business categories.

Example:

SELECT payment_id, amount,
CASE
  WHEN amount >= 1000 THEN 'High Value'
  WHEN amount >= 500 THEN 'Medium Value'
  ELSE 'Low Value'
END AS payment_value_category
FROM payments;

Business explanation:
This classifies payments by value.
High-value failed payments can be prioritised.

Another example:

SELECT payment_id, status,
CASE
  WHEN status = 'SUCCESS' THEN 'Completed'
  WHEN status = 'FAILED' THEN 'Needs Investigation'
  WHEN status = 'PENDING' THEN 'Awaiting Processing'
  ELSE 'Unknown'
END AS business_status
FROM payments;

Interview answer:

CASE WHEN is useful when raw system values need to be converted into business-friendly categories, priority labels, or reporting fields.

17. Date filtering and timestamps

Most real investigations are time-based.

Business questions:
What failed today?
What changed after a release?
Which payments are stuck?
Which records were created during an incident window?

Example:

SELECT payment_id, payment_reference, status, payment_date
FROM payments
WHERE payment_date >= '2026-06-01'
AND payment_date < '2026-07-01';

This is often safer than BETWEEN when timestamps are involved.

Reason:
If payment_date includes hours, minutes, and seconds, using less than the next period start avoids missing records from late on the final day.

Interview answer:

For date-time fields, I prefer using greater than or equal to the start date and less than the next period start. It avoids missing records when timestamps are included.

18. Pending payments older than the expected window

Business rule:
Payments should not remain pending longer than the agreed processing window.

Query example:

SELECT payment_id, payment_reference, customer_id, amount, status, payment_date
FROM payments
WHERE status = 'PENDING'
AND payment_date < CURRENT_DATE - INTERVAL '2 days';

The exact syntax depends on the database.
PostgreSQL, MySQL, SQL Server, and Oracle use different date functions.

Business explanation:
This identifies stuck payments.

Possible causes:
Missing gateway callback.
Failed status update.
Batch process failure.
Integration timeout.
Manual review delay.

Interview answer:

The exact syntax depends on the database, but the logic is to filter pending records older than the accepted processing window and then investigate shared patterns.

19. Duplicate detection

Duplicate data is common in payment systems.
It can be caused by retry logic, browser refresh, double-clicking, API callback duplication, missing idempotency, migration errors, or manual upload mistakes.

Duplicate payment reference query:

SELECT payment_reference, COUNT(*) AS reference_count
FROM payments
GROUP BY payment_reference
HAVING COUNT(*) > 1;

Business explanation:
This finds payment references appearing more than once.

Impact:
Duplicate payments can cause customer complaints, refunds, finance errors, and reconciliation issues.

Duplicate customer email query:

SELECT email, COUNT(*) AS email_count
FROM customers
GROUP BY email
HAVING COUNT(*) > 1;

Impact:
Duplicate customer records can cause KYC problems, support confusion, and inaccurate reporting.

Interview answer:

Duplicate detection is important because duplicate records can create financial, operational, customer, and compliance risk.

20. Orphan records

An orphan record is a child record without a valid parent.

Example:
A payment has customer_id, but that customer_id does not exist in the customers table.

Query:

SELECT p.payment_id, p.customer_id, p.amount, p.status
FROM payments p
LEFT JOIN customers c
ON p.customer_id = c.customer_id
WHERE c.customer_id IS NULL;

Business explanation:
This identifies payments not linked to valid customers.

Impact:
Reporting issues.
Support confusion.
Compliance risk.
Broken customer ownership.

Interview answer:

I use LEFT JOIN to identify missing parent-child relationships. This is useful for data quality checks, migration validation, and system investigation.

21. Subqueries

A subquery is a query inside another query.

Example:
Find payments above average amount.

SELECT payment_id, amount, status
FROM payments
WHERE amount > (
  SELECT AVG(amount)
  FROM payments
);

Business explanation:
This identifies unusually high-value payments compared with average.

Another example:
Find customers with successful payments.

SELECT customer_id, customer_name, country
FROM customers
WHERE customer_id IN (
  SELECT customer_id
  FROM payments
  WHERE status = 'SUCCESS'
);

Interview answer:

I use subqueries when one query result is needed to filter another dataset.

22. EXISTS

EXISTS checks whether a related record exists.

Example:
Find customers who have at least one successful payment.

SELECT c.customer_id, c.customer_name, c.country
FROM customers c
WHERE EXISTS (
  SELECT 1
  FROM payments p
  WHERE p.customer_id = c.customer_id
  AND p.status = 'SUCCESS'
);

Business explanation:
This returns customers who have successful payments.

Interview answer:

EXISTS is useful when I only need to check whether related records exist, not necessarily return their details.

23. CTEs

CTE means Common Table Expression.
It starts with WITH.
It makes complex SQL easier to read.

Example:

WITH failed_payments AS (
  SELECT customer_id, COUNT(*) AS failed_count
  FROM payments
  WHERE status = 'FAILED'
  GROUP BY customer_id
)
SELECT c.customer_name, c.country, f.failed_count
FROM customers c
JOIN failed_payments f
ON c.customer_id = f.customer_id;

Business explanation:
First, calculate failed payment count by customer.
Then join that result to customer details.

Interview answer:

I use CTEs to break complex analysis into readable steps. This is useful for analyst work because other team members may need to understand and review the logic.

24. COALESCE

COALESCE returns the first non-null value.

Example:

COALESCE(failed_payments, 0).

This means:
If failed_payments is NULL, show zero.

Business use:
In reports, missing values may need to appear as zero instead of blank.

Interview answer:

I use COALESCE when I need to replace NULL values with a default value, such as zero for reporting or calculations.

25. Window functions

Window functions calculate across related rows while keeping row-level detail.

Example:
Rank payments by amount.

SELECT payment_id, customer_id, amount,
RANK() OVER (ORDER BY amount DESC) AS amount_rank
FROM payments;

Business explanation:
This identifies the largest transactions.

Rank payments within each customer:

SELECT payment_id, customer_id, amount,
RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY customer_id ORDER BY amount DESC) AS customer_payment_rank
FROM payments;

Business explanation:
This shows each customer’s highest-value payments.

Interview answer:

Window functions are useful when I need ranking, sequencing, running totals, or comparisons while keeping row-level transaction detail.

26. ROW_NUMBER for duplicate analysis

ROW_NUMBER can help identify duplicate records.

Example:

WITH duplicate_check AS (
  SELECT payment_id, payment_reference, payment_date,
  ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY payment_reference ORDER BY payment_date) AS row_num
  FROM payments
)
SELECT *
FROM duplicate_check
WHERE row_num > 1;

Business explanation:
This keeps the first record and shows additional records with the same payment reference.

Use cases:
Duplicate investigation.
Migration validation.
Retry issue analysis.
Idempotency checks.

Interview answer:

ROW_NUMBER is useful when I need to identify repeated records and separate the first occurrence from later duplicates.

27. UNION and UNION ALL

UNION combines results and removes duplicates.
UNION ALL combines results and keeps duplicates.

Example:

SELECT customer_id, email FROM customers_uk
UNION
SELECT customer_id, email FROM customers_ireland;

Business explanation:
This combines customer lists from two regions and removes duplicate rows.

Interview answer:

I use UNION when I need combined unique results. I use UNION ALL when duplicates should be preserved or when duplicate removal is not required.

28. DISTINCT

DISTINCT returns unique values.

Example:

SELECT DISTINCT status
FROM payments;

Business explanation:
This shows all unique payment statuses.

Use case:
Before testing or migration, I can check whether unexpected statuses exist.

Example expected statuses:
SUCCESS.
FAILED.
PENDING.
CANCELLED.

If UNKNOWN appears unexpectedly, that may need investigation.

Interview answer:

DISTINCT is useful for data profiling because it shows which unique values exist in a field.

29. Data quality checks

Data quality checks are important for Business Systems Analyst roles.

Common checks include:
Missing mandatory values.
Duplicate records.
Invalid statuses.
Orphan records.
Amount mismatches.
Unexpected date values.
Negative amounts.
Currency mismatch.
Invalid country codes.

Example missing fields:

SELECT customer_id, customer_name, email, country
FROM customers
WHERE email IS NULL
OR country IS NULL;

Example negative amount:

SELECT payment_id, amount
FROM payments
WHERE amount < 0;

Business explanation:
Poor data quality can cause failed workflows, reporting errors, customer issues, compliance problems, and UAT failures.

Interview answer:

Before migration or release, I would check missing values, duplicates, invalid statuses, orphan records, mismatches, and unusual date or amount values.

30. Data migration validation

Data migration means moving data from an old system to a new system.

SQL helps validate completeness and correctness.

Common checks:
Record count matches.
Total amount matches.
Mandatory fields are populated.
No unexpected duplicates.
Key relationships are preserved.
Statuses are mapped correctly.
Sample records match.

Example:

SELECT COUNT(*) AS old_payment_count FROM old_payments;
SELECT COUNT(*) AS new_payment_count FROM new_payments;

Count alone is not enough.
Check totals:

SELECT SUM(amount) AS old_total_amount FROM old_payments;
SELECT SUM(amount) AS new_total_amount FROM new_payments;

Interview answer:

For migration validation, I would compare record counts, financial totals, mandatory fields, duplicates, relationships, statuses, and sample records. Count proves records moved, but totals and relationships prove they moved correctly.

31. Reporting validation

Business reports must match source system logic.

Scenario:
A dashboard says total successful payment amount for June is one million euros.
I need to validate it.

Query:

SELECT SUM(amount) AS total_successful_amount
FROM payments
WHERE status = 'SUCCESS'
AND payment_date >= '2026-06-01'
AND payment_date < '2026-07-01';

If the result does not match the dashboard, possible reasons include:
Different date logic.
Timezone differences.
Different status filters.
Refunds included or excluded.
Settlement date used instead of payment date.
Currency conversion.
Duplicate records.
Report refresh delay.
Data pipeline issue.

Interview answer:

I would not simply say the report is wrong. I would compare the report’s business rules with the source-system logic and identify exactly where the difference comes from.

32. Production incident investigation

SQL is useful during incidents.

Scenario:
Payment failures increased between 10 AM and 11 AM.

Query:

SELECT status, COUNT(*) AS payment_count, SUM(amount) AS total_amount
FROM payments
WHERE payment_date >= '2026-06-17 10:00:00'
AND payment_date < '2026-06-17 11:00:00'
GROUP BY status;

Then break down by payment method:

SELECT payment_method, status, COUNT(*) AS payment_count
FROM payments
WHERE payment_date >= '2026-06-17 10:00:00'
AND payment_date < '2026-06-17 11:00:00'
GROUP BY payment_method, status;

Incident communication:
Between 10 AM and 11 AM, there were 120 failed payments with total value of 18,500 euros. Failures were concentrated in card payments. Bank transfers were not affected.

Interview answer:

SQL helps move incident communication from vague issue to measured business impact.

33. SQL and UAT

UAT means User Acceptance Testing.
SQL supports UAT by checking backend data against expected business outcomes.

Requirement:
When payment status is SUCCESS, settlement must exist.

Validation query:

SELECT p.payment_id, p.payment_reference, p.status, s.settlement_id
FROM payments p
LEFT JOIN settlements s
ON p.payment_id = s.payment_id
WHERE p.status = 'SUCCESS'
AND s.settlement_id IS NULL;

If zero records return, the rule passes.
If records return, the rule fails or needs investigation.

Defect wording:
Expected result: successful payment should create settlement record.
Actual result: payment status is SUCCESS but settlement record is missing.
Evidence: SQL result showing payment ID and missing settlement ID.
Business impact: finance operations cannot reconcile the payment.

Interview answer:

SQL helps me validate whether the backend data confirms the business process. It is especially useful when the screen shows one thing but the database needs to confirm the real transaction state.

34. SQL and stakeholder communication

Business users do not need raw SQL.
They need meaning.

Technical result:
LEFT JOIN returned NULL settlement IDs.

Business explanation:
I found successful payments where settlement records have not been created. These payments may need finance operations investigation before reconciliation can be completed.

Technical result:
COUNT of failed payments increased after deployment.

Business explanation:
Failed payment volume increased after the release, especially for card payments.

Technical result:
Duplicate payment references exist.

Business explanation:
The same payment reference appears more than once, which may indicate duplicate transaction creation or retry-handling issues.

Interview answer:

A key part of the Business Analyst role is translating technical findings into clear business language.

35. Database transactions

A database transaction is a group of operations that should complete reliably together.

Payment example:
Insert payment record.
Update status.
Create audit log.
Send event to settlement service.
Create ledger entry.

If one part fails, the system may need rollback or compensation logic.

Basic concept:
ACID means Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, and Durability.
I do not need to go too deep unless asked.

Interview answer:

A transaction is a controlled set of database operations that should complete reliably. In payment systems, transaction handling is important because partial updates can create inconsistent records, such as a payment marked successful but missing settlement or ledger entry.

36. Indexes and performance awareness

An index helps the database find records faster.
It is like an index in a book.

Common indexed fields:
Primary keys.
Foreign keys.
Payment reference.
Customer ID.
Payment date.
Status.

If payment_id is indexed, joining payments and settlements may be faster.
If payment_date is indexed, date filtering may be faster.

Good analyst habits:
Avoid SELECT star when unnecessary.
Use clear join conditions.
Use WHERE filters.
Filter date ranges carefully.
Avoid joining large tables without a clear key.

Interview answer:

I am not a database administrator, but I understand that indexes can improve query performance on large tables. As an analyst, I try to write readable queries using proper filters and join keys.

37. Views and stored procedures

A view is a saved query that behaves like a virtual table.
It can simplify complex joins for reporting or analysis.

Example:
A successful_payments_view may already join customers, payments, and settlements.

A stored procedure is a saved set of SQL logic that can be executed.
It may process payments, update statuses, run batch jobs, or generate reports.

Interview answer:

I understand that views can provide a reusable business-friendly layer over complex data. I also understand that stored procedures can contain reusable database logic. I may not write complex stored procedures myself, but I can work with technical teams to understand their purpose, validate outputs, and investigate issues when they affect business data.

38. API and database flow

Modern systems often use APIs and databases together.

Payment flow example:
Customer clicks pay on the UI.
Frontend sends request to API.
API validates the request.
API writes payment record to database.
Payment gateway processes the request.
Gateway sends callback.
API updates payment status.
Settlement service creates settlement record.
Reporting service reads the data.

Business Analyst value:
A screen issue may come from frontend, API, database, integration, gateway, or reporting layer.
SQL helps confirm what happened in the database.

Interview answer:

Scenario 1: Successful payment with no settlement

Interviewer asks:

Pause.

Ideal answer:

Scenario 16: WHERE versus HAVING

Interviewer asks:

Think.

Ideal answer:

WHERE p.status = 'FAILED'

WHERE filters only failed payments.
GROUP BY summarises them by country.
HAVING keeps only countries with more than ten failed payments.

2. Payment lifecycle overview

A payment lifecycle usually includes several stages.

Payment initiation.
Validation.
Authorisation.
Capture.
Processing.
Settlement.
Reconciliation.
Reporting.
Refund or chargeback if needed.

Payment initiation means the customer starts the payment.
Validation checks whether required fields are present and rules are satisfied.
Authorisation checks whether the payment can be approved.
Capture confirms the merchant or platform wants to take the funds.
Settlement is movement or recording of funds between parties.
Reconciliation checks whether internal and external records match.
Reporting summarises transactions for operations, finance, risk, and management.

Interview answer:

When investigating payment issues, I think across the lifecycle. A payment can fail at validation, authorisation, capture, settlement, reconciliation, or reporting stage. SQL helps identify which stage has inconsistent or missing data.

3. Payment statuses

Common payment statuses may include initiated, authorised, captured, success, failed, pending, cancelled, settled, refunded, partially refunded, disputed, and chargeback.

The exact statuses depend on the system.

A Business Analyst should not assume status meaning.
I should ask:
What does SUCCESS mean?
Does SUCCESS mean authorised or fully captured?
Does SETTLED mean funds have moved or only settlement record created?
Can PENDING remain valid for a defined period?
What are allowed status transitions?

Interview answer:

Payment statuses must be clearly defined because reporting, UAT, reconciliation, and customer communication depend on them. I would validate not only the current status, but also whether the status transition follows the expected workflow.

4. Authorisation, capture, and settlement

Authorisation means the payment provider or issuer approves the transaction.
Capture means the merchant confirms the funds should be taken.
Settlement means funds are moved or recorded for financial reconciliation.

In some systems, authorisation and capture happen together.
In other systems, they are separate.

This matters because a payment may be authorised but not captured, captured but not settled, or settled with a different amount due to fees, refunds, or FX.

Interview answer:

I would be careful not to treat all successful-looking payments the same. I would clarify whether success means authorised, captured, or settled. This affects SQL validation, UAT rules, and reconciliation checks.

5. Reconciliation

Reconciliation means checking that records match across systems or stages.

Examples:
Payment amount should match settlement amount.
Settlement records should match ledger entries.
Internal records should match payment provider files.
Report totals should match source tables.
Refunds and chargebacks should be reflected in net revenue.

SQL helps reconciliation by finding mismatches, missing records, duplicates, and totals.

Interview answer:

Reconciliation is the process of proving that financial records agree across systems. In a payments context, I would use SQL to compare payment records, settlement records, ledger entries, and reporting totals to identify missing or mismatched records.

Part 3B — FinTech and RegTech Interview Q&A Bank

This section is for domain recall. I should listen repeatedly until the answers sound natural.

Domain Q1: What is a payment lifecycle?
Answer: A payment lifecycle is the journey from customer initiation through validation, authorisation, capture, settlement, reconciliation, reporting, and possible refund or chargeback. In interviews, I should explain that each stage can create data and each stage can fail differently.

Domain Q2: What is settlement?
Answer: Settlement is the stage where funds are moved or recorded between parties. In system terms, settlement records should link back to payment records and support reconciliation, finance reporting, and ledger accuracy.

Domain Q3: What is reconciliation?
Answer: Reconciliation is checking that records match across systems or stages. For example, payment amount should match settlement amount, settlement totals should match ledger entries, and reports should match source data.

Domain Q4: What is a ledger?
Answer: A ledger is the accounting record of financial movements. A payment, refund, fee, or chargeback may create ledger entries. In regulated systems, ledger accuracy is critical for finance and audit.

Domain Q5: What is a chargeback?
Answer: A chargeback is a customer dispute raised through a bank or card issuer. It can affect revenue, risk, operations, and reporting. Systems need to track chargeback status, amount, evidence, deadlines, and outcome.

Domain Q6: What is KYC?
Answer: KYC means Know Your Customer. It verifies customer identity and eligibility. In systems, it involves onboarding data, verification status, risk scoring, evidence, audit trail, and sometimes manual review.

Domain Q7: What is AML?
Answer: AML means Anti-Money Laundering. It includes controls to detect and prevent suspicious financial activity. Systems may support sanctions screening, transaction monitoring, risk scoring, alerts, and case management.

Domain Q8: What is RegTech?
Answer: RegTech is technology that helps firms manage regulatory obligations, controls, evidence, monitoring, reporting, and compliance workflows. Examples include control dashboards, incident reporting, KYC workflows, and DORA evidence registers.

Domain Q9: Why does audit trail matter?
Answer: Audit trail matters because regulated firms need to prove who changed what, when, and why. It supports investigation, compliance, operational control, and accountability.

Domain Q10: What is operational resilience?
Answer: Operational resilience means the ability to continue delivering important services during disruption. In financial systems, this includes monitoring, incident handling, recovery, third-party risk, testing, and post-incident reconciliation.

Domain Q11: What is DORA?
Answer: DORA is the EU Digital Operational Resilience Act. It applies to many financial entities and focuses on ICT risk management, incident reporting, resilience testing, ICT third-party risk, and evidence.

Domain Q12: How does DORA relate to BA work?
Answer: DORA creates business analysis work around incident workflows, reporting requirements, third-party registers, control evidence, risk data, dashboards, UAT, and process documentation.

Domain Q13: What is PSD2?
Answer: PSD2 is the EU payment services framework that shaped payment services, open banking, strong customer authentication, and payment institution regulation.

Domain Q14: What are PSD3 and PSR?
Answer: PSD3 and PSR are proposed EU updates to payment services rules. The direction is stronger consumer protection, security, competition, and modernisation of digital payments.

Domain Q15: What is open banking?
Answer: Open banking allows authorised third parties to access account data or initiate payments with customer consent through secure APIs. It requires consent tracking, authentication, audit logs, and data protection.

Domain Q16: What is open finance?
Answer: Open finance extends secure, consent-based data sharing beyond payment accounts to a wider range of financial services data. FiDA is the EU proposal in this area.

Domain Q17: What is MiCA?
Answer: MiCA is the EU Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation. It creates a regulatory framework for crypto-assets and crypto-asset service providers, including governance, consumer protection, and authorisation expectations.

Domain Q18: What is GDPR relevance in FinTech?
Answer: FinTech systems process personal and financial data, so GDPR principles affect access control, data minimisation, retention, auditability, consent where relevant, and secure data sharing.

Domain Q19: How do SQL and RegTech connect?
Answer: SQL can produce control evidence and exception reports. For example, it can identify high-value payments without risk review, missing settlement records, or KYC-unapproved customers making restricted transactions.

Domain Q20: How do SQL and FinTech operations connect?
Answer: SQL helps operations monitor failed payments, pending items, missing settlements, chargebacks, refunds, and high-value exceptions. It turns system data into action lists.

Domain Q21: What is safeguarding in payment/e-money context?
Answer: Safeguarding generally refers to protecting customer funds according to regulatory expectations. As a BA, I should know it creates requirements around account segregation, reporting, reconciliations, controls, and evidence, though legal detail belongs to compliance experts.

Domain Q22: What is strong customer authentication?
Answer: Strong customer authentication is a security concept under payment regulation involving robust customer verification. System impact can include authentication flows, exemptions, failure handling, customer messaging, and audit logs.

Domain Q23: What is fraud monitoring?
Answer: Fraud monitoring uses rules, models, alerts, and review workflows to detect suspicious transactions. Systems need data quality, explainable rules, case management, and false-positive handling.

Domain Q24: What is payment exception management?
Answer: Exception management is the process for handling payments that do not follow the normal path, such as failed, pending, mismatched, duplicate, disputed, or manually adjusted payments.

Domain Q25: What is a control?
Answer: A control is a process, rule, check, or approval that reduces risk. Example: high-value payments require review. SQL can test whether the control operated by finding high-value payments with no review record.

Domain Q26: What is control evidence?
Answer: Control evidence is proof that a control was performed. It may be an approval record, audit log, SQL extract, dashboard, ticket, report, or UAT result.

Domain Q27: Why are requirements different in regulated systems?
Answer: Requirements need to be testable, auditable, and linked to controls. Vague requirements create operational and compliance risk. Good requirements define data, workflow, exceptions, ownership, and evidence.

Domain Q28: What is an incident workflow?
Answer: An incident workflow includes detection, logging, classification, escalation, investigation, communication, resolution, post-incident review, and evidence. SQL can quantify impact and affected records.

Domain Q29: What is third-party risk?
Answer: Third-party risk comes from external providers such as payment gateways, cloud platforms, KYC vendors, fraud tools, and API providers. Requirements should cover failures, retries, SLAs, monitoring, logs, and fallback processes.

Domain Q30: What is a material outsourcing or ICT provider issue?
Answer: In regulated environments, important third-party services need visibility, governance, risk assessment, monitoring, and evidence. BA work may include registers, workflows, reporting, and incident processes.

Domain Q31: How should I explain FinTech domain in interviews?
Answer: I should say financial systems are controlled workflows around money, data, customers, risk, and evidence. My role is to understand the process, validate data, support UAT, and explain impact clearly.

Domain Q32: How should I explain RegTech domain in interviews?
Answer: I should say RegTech translates regulatory obligations into systems, workflows, controls, data fields, reports, dashboards, audit trails, and evidence.

Domain Q33: How do payment systems fail?
Answer: They can fail at validation, authorisation, capture, callback, settlement, ledger posting, reporting, refund, or chargeback stages. A strong analyst identifies which stage failed.

Domain Q34: What is an error code useful for?
Answer: Error codes help group failures and identify root causes. Missing or vague error codes make operational investigation harder.

Domain Q35: What is payment idempotency?
Answer: Idempotency means repeated requests should not create duplicate effects. In payments, it helps prevent duplicate charges when retries or callbacks happen.

Domain Q36: What is a payment gateway?
Answer: A payment gateway connects the merchant/platform to payment networks or providers. Gateway issues can cause authorisation failures, callback delays, or settlement problems.

Domain Q37: What is a batch process?
Answer: A batch process runs jobs at scheduled times, such as settlement creation, report generation, reconciliation imports, or file processing. Delays or failures can create missing records or stale reports.

Domain Q38: What is an API callback?
Answer: A callback is a message from an external provider back to the system, often updating payment status. Missing or duplicate callbacks can create status and settlement issues.

Domain Q39: What is an audit-friendly defect?
Answer: An audit-friendly defect includes expected result, actual result, record IDs, evidence, impact, severity, date/time, and retest outcome.

Domain Q40: What is the best final interview positioning?
Answer: I am a FinTech and regulated systems Business Analyst candidate with SQL validation, UAT, payment workflow, reconciliation, incident, and control evidence understanding.

Part 3C — Integrated Case Studies for Interview Storytelling

Case Study 1 — Successful payments missing settlements

Business problem: Finance operations reports that daily settlement total is lower than successful payment total.

Analysis path:
First, confirm the business rule: every successful captured payment should create a settlement record after the settlement batch.
Second, identify source tables: payments, settlements, possibly status history, gateway callbacks, and ledger entries.
Third, use LEFT JOIN to find successful payments without settlements.
Fourth, summarise by payment date, method, gateway, currency, count, and total amount.
Fifth, check whether missing settlements started after a release, batch job failure, or provider outage.

Business impact:
Finance cannot reconcile those transactions. Reporting may be understated. Customers may have completed payments, but finance records are incomplete.

Interview closing:
I would provide a clear defect or incident summary with affected IDs, total value, time window, suspected process stage, and recommended next checks.

Case Study 2 — Report total mismatch

Business problem: A dashboard says successful payments for June total 1 million euros, but finance source query shows 980,000 euros.

Analysis path:
Clarify report definition. Is it gross payment amount, net amount after refunds, settled amount, captured amount, or ledger amount?
Check date logic. Is the report using payment date, settlement date, or reporting date?
Check timezone. Does the report use UTC while finance uses local time?
Check status logic. Does the report include authorised or only captured payments?
Check refunds, chargebacks, currency conversion, and duplicate records.

Business impact:
Incorrect reports can affect management decisions, financial reporting, operational monitoring, and trust in dashboards.

Interview closing:
I would compare source-system logic and report logic, identify the exact rule difference, and document the agreed definition for future reporting.

Case Study 3 — Payment failure spike after release

Business problem: Customer support says payment failures increased after a release.

Analysis path:
Get release timestamp. Compare failure rate before and after the release using equal time windows. Break down by payment method, country, currency, gateway, error code, and device if available. Check whether total volume changed. Review status history and gateway callbacks.

Business impact:
If the spike affects live payments, there may be revenue loss, customer complaints, operational workload, and potential incident escalation.

Interview closing:
A strong answer does not only say failures increased. It says failure rate increased from one percent to seven percent after the release, mainly for card payments through one gateway, affecting 400 transactions and 32,000 euros in attempted value.

Case Study 4 — KYC control failure

Business problem: Compliance asks whether any unapproved customers were allowed to transact.

Analysis path:
Join customer KYC status to payment records. Filter payments where customer KYC is not approved or missing. Check payment status and transaction amount. Summarise exceptions by date, value, country, and onboarding channel. Check whether workflow rule was bypassed or data was stale.

Business impact:
This can be a compliance, control, and operational risk issue.

Interview closing:
SQL can support control testing by identifying exceptions to the expected rule and producing evidence for compliance review.

Case Study 5 — DORA-style incident evidence

Business problem: A regulated firm needs evidence for an ICT-related payment disruption.

Analysis path:
Identify incident time window and impacted service. Use SQL to count failed payments, pending payments, missing settlements, and affected customers. Summarise by payment method, country, provider, and value. Link SQL evidence to incident ticket, timeline, root cause, remediation, and post-incident control improvements.

Business impact:
Regulated firms need incident evidence, not only technical explanations. Good data supports internal governance, supervisory reporting where applicable, customer communication, and remediation tracking.

Interview closing:
As a Business Systems Analyst, I would help define the data needed for incident classification, impact assessment, reporting, and post-incident evidence.
